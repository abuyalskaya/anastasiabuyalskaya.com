Empirical

“What Builds and Breaks Exercise Habits: How State Variables Affect Your Likelihood of Going to the Gym” with C. Camerer, A. Duckworth, K. Milkman

Abstract: Understanding how individuals build and break habits – particularly healthy habits like regular exercise – is a crucial component of behavior change. We partner with a large gym chain to obtain a panel dataset on ~70,000 individuals spanning up to 12 years of gym attendance. We then apply computational modeling to identify moderators associated with more habitual attendance, as well as to understand how exogenous shocks (such as a significant weather changes) are likely to disrupt habitual behavior. These results have important implications for how healthy habits might be built and sustained through time.

Experimental

“Crime and (Optimal) Punishment” with M. Agranov

Abstract: We study experimentally how revealing different information about a punishment distribution affects crime deterrence. In our laboratory experiments, we hold the expected value of punishment equal across treatments, while changing the framing of the same punishment scheme. We use an incentive-compatible elicitation method to observe lying behavior across subjects (`committing the crime in the lab’), and quantify the extent to which this lying behavior responds to the information provided to subjects regarding the probability of being monitored as well as the punishment in case one is caught lying. In line with theories of risk and ambiguity aversion, we find that punishments which have equivalent expected values but are perceived to be riskier are more effective at deterring lying behavior. These results have important implications for how institutions, which seek to deter crime, should frame potential punishments.

Research Interests

Behavioral Science, Behavioral Finance


I have advised venture, hedge fund, and money management firms and investors. I speak at industry conferences on behavioral finance topics including cognitive bias, investor behaviors to foster and discourage, and strategies to improve overall performance and business development.

I formed the Behavioral Finance team at BlackRock Asset Management. The initial focus was to broaden our risk metrics to include manager (human) risks. We grew the team to focus on team risks, provided decision-making guidelines (quantitative and qualitative) across a range of asset classes, created content for a firm-wide behavioral finance course, and informed talent management both for existing staff and for recruiting.

My product development experience includes building a platform to tease out behavioral components from portfolio manager transaction performance, and a framework to objectivize, analyze, and improve individual decision-making and group dynamic decision-making of finance teams.
